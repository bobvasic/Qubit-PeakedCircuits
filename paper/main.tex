\begin{abstract}
Peaked quantum circuits---circuits engineered so that a single bitstring dominates the output probability distribution---serve as a testbed for verifiable quantum advantage. We report on a systematic attempt to solve ten peaked circuits (P1--P10) ranging from 4 to 62 qubits, presented in the BlueQubit ``Peaked Portal'' hackathon. Circuits P1--P3 were solved exactly using statevector simulation and matrix product state (MPS) methods. For the remaining seven circuits (P4--P10, 40--62 qubits, 888--5{,}096 two-qubit gates), we exhaustively explored classical MPS simulation at bond dimensions up to 2{,}048, Reverse Cuthill--McKee qubit reordering, cloud-based GPU tensor networks, marginal probability reconstruction, inverse circuit verification, real quantum hardware execution on IBM Quantum (Heron r2, 156 qubits) and BlueQubit QPU, NVIDIA H100 GPU simulation, and alternative tensor network libraries (quimb/cotengra). All approaches for P4--P10 returned uniformly random measurement outcomes. Deep structural analysis of P4 revealed it to be a 48-qubit \emph{ring} (mislabeled as ``cross'') built from 2{,}543 KAK-decomposed CZ sandwiches. We analyze failure modes, quantify computational resource requirements, and discuss implications for quantum advantage benchmarking.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Demonstrating quantum computational advantage---the ability of a quantum device to perform a task intractable for any classical computer---remains a central goal of quantum computing research~\cite{arute2019quantum,zhong2020quantum}. Peaked quantum circuits provide one framework for such demonstrations: a circuit $U$ is constructed such that $|\!\braket{x|U|0^n}\!|^2 \gg 2^{-n}$ for a single ``peaked'' bitstring $x$, while all other bitstrings have approximately uniform probability~\cite{aaronson2017complexity}.

The BlueQubit ``Peaked Portal'' hackathon~\cite{bluequbit2025hackathon} presented ten such circuits of increasing difficulty, challenging participants to identify each circuit's peak bitstring. The circuits span diverse topologies (linear, grid, heavy-hex, ring, all-to-all, dense) and range from trivial (4 qubits) to classically intractable (62 qubits with thousands of entangling gates).

This paper provides a comprehensive technical report of our approach, documenting both successes and failures across \textbf{nine distinct simulation platforms}. We solved P1--P3 using exact statevector simulation and MPS methods. For P4--P10, despite deploying every accessible simulation strategy over multiple days of computation, all methods returned noise---uniformly random bitstrings with no discernible peaked structure.

\subsection{Topology Analysis}
\label{sec:topology}

Understanding the coupling map topology is critical for selecting simulation strategies, particularly for MPS-based methods that exploit low entanglement across one-dimensional cuts. We analyzed each circuit's coupling graph using NetworkX~\cite{hagberg2008exploring}.

Figure~\ref{fig:topologies} illustrates the six distinct topology classes encountered. A key discovery was that \textbf{two circuits had mislabeled topologies}: P4 (labeled ``Cross,'' actually a ring) and P9 (labeled ``Circular,'' actually densely connected with degrees 33--42).

\label{fig:topologies}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Deep Structural Analysis of P4}
\label{sec:p4analysis}

Circuit P4 received the most intensive analysis. Key findings:

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{True topology:} 48-qubit ring (cycle graph $C_{48}$) with exactly 48 edges. Every qubit has degree exactly 2, connecting only to its two ring neighbors. There are no cross-links, hubs, or shortcuts.
    \item \textbf{Gate structure:} The 5{,}096 CZ gates decompose into \textbf{2{,}543 CZ-u3-CZ sandwiches} (KAK decomposition of arbitrary two-qubit unitaries) plus 10 standalone CZ gates.
    \item \textbf{Per-edge loading:} Each of the 48 ring edges carries approximately 106 CZ gates (range: 104--108).
    \item \textbf{Macro structure:} Three phases---staircase buildup (introducing pairs sequentially), $\sim$40 steady-state sweeps across half the ring, and staircase wind-down.
    \item \textbf{No cancellations:} Despite even CZ counts on many edges, every CZ pair has non-trivial u3 rotations between them, making them irreducible.
\end{itemize}

The circuit consists of 2{,}543 such sandwiches plus 10 standalone CZ gates.}

\subsection{Exact Statevector Simulation}
\label{sec:statevector}

For circuits with $n \leq 34$ qubits, exact statevector simulation is feasible, requiring $\bigO(2^n)$ memory. We used BlueQubit's cloud \texttt{cpu} device, which provides full statevector access.

\textbf{Results:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \circuit{P1} (4 qubits): Solved in 12\,ms. Peak bitstring \texttt{1001} with probability \textbf{66.93\%}.
    \item \circuit{P2} (28 qubits): Solved in 29.2\,s. Peak bitstring \texttt{1100101101100011011000011100} with probability \textbf{34.88\%}.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Matrix Product State Simulation}
\label{sec:mps}

Matrix product states~\cite{schollwock2011density,orus2014practical} represent an $n$-qubit state as a chain of tensors:
\begin{equation}
\ket{\psi} = \sum_{i_1,\ldots,i_n} A^{[1]}_{i_1} A^{[2]}_{i_2} \cdots A^{[n]}_{i_n} \ket{i_1 i_2 \cdots i_n}
\end{equation}
where each $A^{[k]}_{i_k}$ is a $\bd_{k-1} \times \bd_k$ matrix. The maximum bond dimension $\bd_{\max}$ controls accuracy vs.\ cost: simulation scales as $\bigO(n \cdot \bd_{\max}^3)$ per gate.

We used Qiskit Aer's MPS simulator~\cite{qiskit2024} (locally) and BlueQubit's cloud \texttt{mps.cpu} device with bond dimensions $\bd \in \{64, 128, 256, 512, 1024, 2048\}$.

\textbf{Results:}
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \circuit{P3} (44 qubits, grid): \textbf{Solved} at $\bd = 256$ via BlueQubit \texttt{mps.cpu} in 2.1\,minutes. Peak bitstring found with \textbf{11.01\%} probability (1{,}101 of 10{,}000 shots).
    \item \circuit{P4--P10}: All bond dimensions up to $\bd = 2{,}048$ returned uniformly random outputs---10{,}000 unique bitstrings from 10{,}000 shots.
\end{itemize}

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{figures/fig3_mps_scaling.pdf}
\caption{MPS runtime scaling for P4 (48 qubits, RCM-reordered) as a function of bond dimension. All runs returned noise despite super-polynomial growth in compute time. Dashed line shows extrapolated scaling.}
\label{fig:mps_scaling}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Reverse Cuthill--McKee Qubit Reordering}
\label{sec:rcm}

The Reverse Cuthill--McKee (RCM) algorithm~\cite{cuthill1969reducing} reorders vertices of a sparse graph to minimize bandwidth, potentially improving MPS performance by making entanglement more local in the tensor chain.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/fig4_rcm_bandwidth.pdf}
\caption{RCM bandwidth reduction. P4 achieved a 23.5$\times$ improvement (47 $\to$ 2), effectively linearizing the ring. Dense topologies (P5, P6, P9) showed minimal improvement.}
\label{fig:rcm}
\end{figure}

\textbf{Key finding:} Despite reducing P4's bandwidth from 47 to 2 (making it a near-perfect 1D chain), the 5{,}096 CZ gates still generate entanglement exceeding any feasible bond dimension. At $\bd = 256$ after RCM, the simulation took \textbf{4.4 hours} and returned noise.

\subsection{Cloud Quantum Simulation}
\label{sec:cloud}

BlueQubit provides cloud-based simulation infrastructure:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \texttt{mps.cpu}: CPU-based MPS, free on subscription. Max $\bd = 2{,}048$.
    \item \texttt{mps.gpu}: GPU-accelerated MPS, \$10--17 per job at $\bd = 1024$.
    \item \texttt{tensor-network}: Full tensor network contraction (requires elevated authentication).
\end{itemize}

We submitted $\sim$20 jobs to \texttt{mps.cpu} at $\bd = 256$--$1024$, with runtimes from 19.5\,min (P8, $\bd = 256$) to an estimated 10.4\,hours (P7, $\bd = 1024$). All completed jobs returned noise. One GPU attempt (P8, $\bd = 1024$, \$9.46) was terminated before completion.

\subsection{Marginal Probability Reconstruction}
\label{sec:marginals}

We developed a marginal voting scheme: run $R$ independent low-$\bd$ MPS simulations, compute per-qubit probabilities $p_q = P(\text{qubit } q = 1)$, and reconstruct a candidate by majority vote.

\textbf{Results for P8 (40 qubits):} At $\bd = 64$, average confidence was 0.627 with only 17/40 qubits strongly determined. At $\bd = 128$, confidence \emph{decreased} to 0.599---indicating that insufficient $\bd$ produces structured noise that does not converge to the true marginals.

\subsection{Inverse Circuit Verification}
\label{sec:inverse}

Given a candidate $x$, we verify correctness by checking:
\begin{equation}
P(\text{all zeros}) = |\!\braket{0^n | U^\dagger X_x | 0^n}\!|^2 = |\!\braket{x | U | 0^n}\!|^2
\end{equation}
This tool was built but could not be deployed effectively, as no method produced plausible candidates.

\subsection{Real Quantum Hardware}
\label{sec:hardware}

\paragraph{IBM Quantum (ibm\_fez, Heron r2, 156 qubits).}
We executed both P5 and P4 on IBM's Heron r2 processor~\cite{ibm2024heron}:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{P5} (44 qubits, 1{,}892 CZ): Job \texttt{d6s8kg790okc73es0at0} returned 10{,}000 unique bitstrings---pure decoherence noise.
    \item \textbf{P4} (48 qubits, 5{,}096 CZ): After transpilation with optimization level 3, the circuit expanded to \textbf{46{,}014 gates at depth 1{,}066}. Job \texttt{d6tbr7esh9gc73dig5r0} returned 10{,}000 unique bitstrings---pure noise.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{NVIDIA H100 GPU Simulation}
\label{sec:h100}

We provisioned an NVIDIA H100 GPU instance (94\,GB VRAM) on BlueQubit's cloud infrastructure, which includes NVIDIA cuQuantum~24.08~\cite{nvidia2023cutensornet} pre-installed. MPS simulation at $\bd = 512$ was launched for P4, but the Qiskit Aer MPS backend runs on CPU only and did not utilize the GPU. The instance's compute credits were exhausted before the simulation completed.

This experience highlights a critical lesson: \textbf{having GPU hardware is necessary but not sufficient}---the simulation software must be GPU-native (e.g., cuTensorNet, cuStateVec) to benefit from GPU acceleration.

\subsection{Alternative Tensor Network Methods}
\label{sec:quimb}

We attempted quimb~\cite{gray2018quimb} with cotengra~\cite{gray2021hyper} for full tensor network contraction. The attempt failed due to API incompatibility (quimb 1.12.1's \texttt{TensorNetworkGenVector} lacked the expected \texttt{convert\_to\_mps} method), and cotengra fell back to random contraction order sampling without the recommended hyper-optimizers (kahypar, optuna).

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Entanglement Barrier}

The fundamental barrier is that circuits P4--P10 generate bipartite entanglement entropy exceeding what MPS at any computationally feasible bond dimension can represent. For an MPS with bond dimension $\bd$, the maximum representable entanglement entropy across any bipartition is $S_{\max} = \log_2 \bd$. At $\bd = 1024$, this is only 10 ebits.

Recent work~\cite{bluequbit2025peaked} demonstrates that solving peaked circuits of this scale requires bond dimensions of $\bd \geq 5{,}000$, with the required $\bd$ growing exponentially with circuit depth. Figure~\ref{fig:entanglement} illustrates this barrier.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{figures/fig10_entanglement.pdf}
\caption{Entanglement barrier: estimated required bond dimension vs.\ two-qubit gate count. Solved circuits (green) require modest $\bd$, while unsolved circuits (red) require $\bd$ far exceeding current computational limits. The dashed green line shows the maximum cloud MPS capacity ($\bd = 2048$); the dotted orange line shows estimated H100 GPU limits.}
\label{fig:entanglement}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Why Quantum Hardware Failed}

Current quantum processors lack the gate fidelity and coherence times for circuits of this depth:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Transpilation blowup:} P4 expanded from 15{,}336 to 46{,}014 gates (3.0$\times$) when transpiled for IBM Heron R2, due to basis gate decomposition and routing.
    \item \textbf{Coherence limits:} At depth 1{,}066, the circuit far exceeds the processor's $T_2$ coherence time, causing complete decoherence.
    \item \textbf{Gate limits:} BlueQubit QPU rejects circuits exceeding 20{,}000 gates, which all unsolved circuits surpass after transpilation.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Topology Discovery: P4 is a Ring}

The discovery that P4 is a 48-qubit ring (not a cross) has significant implications:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item Ring topology is \emph{optimal} for MPS---it requires only a single cut to linearize.
    \item RCM reordering of the ring achieves bandwidth 2 (near-perfect 1D chain).
    \item Despite this optimal topology, the \textbf{5{,}096 CZ gates} ($\sim$106 per edge, organized as $\sim$40 sweep layers) generate entanglement that still defeats MPS at $\bd \leq 2{,}048$.
    \item This proves that \textbf{circuit depth, not topology, is the dominant factor} in MPS tractability for peaked circuits.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Comparison with Published Results}

The BlueQubit team's own analysis~\cite{bluequbit2025peaked} confirms that:
\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item MPS methods crack circuits with up to $\sim$600 two-qubit gates at $\bd = 5{,}000$.
    \item Pauli path simulation (PPS) on H100 GPUs handles circuits up to $\sim$388 RZZ gates.
    \item Tensor network states with belief propagation (TNS+BP) can handle all-to-all connectivity but with limited accuracy.
    \item The largest circuits (56 qubits, 2{,}044 two-qubit gates) require under 2 hours of quantum execution versus millions of GPU hours classically---demonstrating genuine quantum advantage.
\end{itemize}

Our results are consistent with these findings: P3 ($\sim$200 two-qubit gates) was solvable, while P8 (888 gates) and beyond were not, placing the boundary between 200 and 888 two-qubit gates for our accessible computational resources.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{GPU-native tensor network contraction:} Using cuTensorNet~\cite{nvidia2023cutensornet} (not MPS via Qiskit Aer) on multi-GPU systems with optimized contraction ordering via cotengra+kahypar. Our H100 attempt failed because MPS runs on CPU; a cuTensorNet implementation would exploit the full 94\,GB GPU memory.

    \item \textbf{Pauli path simulation:} Backward evolution computing $\langle Z_i \rangle$ expectation values directly, as demonstrated in~\cite{bluequbit2025peaked}. This avoids sampling and can determine individual bits of the peak bitstring.

    \item \textbf{Circuit cutting:} Decomposing circuits into smaller subcircuits~\cite{peng2020simulating}, particularly promising for P4's ring topology where a single cut produces a linear chain.

    \item \textbf{Variational optimization:} Using parameterized ansatz (variational MPS or MERA~\cite{vidal2007entanglement}) to directly optimize for the peak bitstring via gradient descent on $|\!\braket{x|U|0^n}\!|^2$.

    \item \textbf{Error-mitigated quantum hardware:} Zero-noise extrapolation or probabilistic error cancellation~\cite{temme2017error} applied to noisy hardware results on next-generation processors with higher gate fidelity.

    \item \textbf{PEPS for 2D topologies:} Projected entangled pair states~\cite{verstraete2004renormalization} for circuits like P3 and P8 with grid topologies, where area-law entanglement scaling may be exploitable.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Conclusions}
\label{sec:conclusions}

We systematically explored nine simulation methods across seven hardware platforms for solving ten peaked quantum circuits. Three circuits (P1--P3) were solved using exact statevector simulation and MPS methods. The remaining seven (P4--P10) resisted all approaches, including MPS at bond dimensions up to 2{,}048, RCM-optimized qubit reordering, cloud GPU simulation, marginal reconstruction, real quantum hardware (IBM Heron R2 with 156 qubits), NVIDIA H100 GPU, and alternative tensor network libraries.

\begin{figure}[H]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{figures/fig6_flowchart.pdf}
\caption{Method selection strategy employed throughout the hackathon, showing the systematic escalation from exact simulation through approximate classical methods to quantum hardware.}
\label{fig:flowchart}
\end{figure}

Three key findings emerge:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{The solvability boundary lies between $\sim$200 and $\sim$888 two-qubit gates} on 40+ qubit circuits. Below this threshold, MPS at moderate bond dimension captures the peak; above it, entanglement exceeds the capacity of all accessible methods.

    \item \textbf{Circuit depth dominates topology.} P4's ring topology is optimal for MPS, yet 5{,}096 CZ gates ($\sim$40 sweep layers of $\sim$106 gates per edge) generate unmanageable entanglement. Topology determines the \emph{floor} of required $\bd$; depth determines the \emph{ceiling}.

    \item \textbf{Current quantum hardware cannot help.} IBM's 156-qubit Heron R2 produced pure noise on circuits with $\geq$1{,}892 CZ gates. The gap between hardware coherence and circuit depth requirements remains orders of magnitude.
\end{enumerate}

These results validate peaked circuits as effective quantum advantage benchmarks: they occupy the regime where classical simulation is demonstrably infeasible with current resources, while remaining verifiable by construction.
